Overview of Analysis

This analysis was conducted to evaluate the potential savings for our company by transitioning to a model where we hire six in-house installers in Maine instead of relying on third-party installers. Maine, being the state with the majority of our installation revenue, was critical to the success of this analysis. Given that our installation order volume varies year to year, the analysis included projections for both low and high volume installation years.

The first step was breaking the state into territories to ensure more manageable and comparable data analysis. By utilizing ERP data pulled from Epicor and Power BI, I generated a heatmap of all orders within the state over the last three years. This helped identify regions that would benefit from in-house installers by minimizing expenses.

Data Sources and Methodology

The data for this analysis was sourced from our internal ERP (Epicor) system, where I pulled installation order details for the past three years. I then created a heatmap to break down the data by zip code, helping us identify the most efficient territories for placing installers. Along with installation data, revenue from orders and expenses incurred from third-party installers were used to establish baseline costs.

Several challenges arose due to how our company tracks expenses and recognizes revenue. First, our expenses for third-party installers are not tracked at a line-item level, making it difficult to compare costs accurately. Additionally, while our company charges customers for both installation and freight, the freight charges also cover travel and supply costs that third-party installers bill under the installation expenses. This led to challenges in accurately comparing costs between the two models.

Cost Breakdown

For third-party providers, major cost components were:

  • Installation charges per project type
  • Regional differences in labor rates
  • Travel expenses for on-site installation work (e.g., travel time, accommodation, per diem)

For the in-house model, the projected costs included:

  • Salaries, benefits, and insurance for new employees
  • Overhead costs for new equipment, tools, and supplies
  • Vehicle purchase and maintenance (for 6 new vehicles)
  • Office staff and technology (computers, printers, and office supplies)

This comparison was crucial to determining whether the transition to an in-house model would lead to significant savings or result in increased costs.

Projected Savings

By shifting installation responsibilities to an in-house team, the analysis projected potential savings in travel expenses, hourly rates on-site, and overall operational costs:

  • Travel Expenses: On a low-volume installation year, savings would be approximately $265,000, and on a high-volume year, savings would increase to $345,000.
  • Hourly Installations: By hiring local union carpenters, we estimated savings of $90,000 in a low-volume year and $142,000 in a high-volume year.

However, after factoring in the additional expenses associated with hiring in-house staff, including salaries, benefits, vehicles, and office staff, the overall savings were offset by an additional $199,000 in operating expenses for low installation years, and $128,000 in high installation years. This led to an initial capital investment of $465,000 for equipment, vehicles, and technology.

Despite initial expectations for significant savings, the analysis ultimately recommended against moving forward with the expansion to Maine due to the projected costs outweighing the savings potential.
